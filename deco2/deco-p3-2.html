Reverse Decoupage

Materials

  1. Clear glass object (plates are common)
    This can be any shape or size as long as it’s made of glass. A more intricate glass will be more difficult. However, if you do choose to use one, it is extra important to use light-weight material and cut/rip it into smaller pieces so that you can get into the groves better.
  2. Primer
    • Polyvine’s multi-surface lacquer is good for any shiny surface

  3. White chalk paint (Optional, but can help your colours pop)
  4. Adhesive/varnish
    • Mod Podge is most common.
    • PVA glue or specialty decoupage glues can also be used.
    • If you intend to eat on your chosen glass dish, use dishwasher-safe Mod Podge.
    WARNING: Do not put your decoupaged glass dish in the dishwasher or microwave.
    Handwash only and only wash the area being eaten on. The side with material should be able to survive a brief rinse provided it is not being scrubbed and it is dried quickly.
  5. Material(s) you’re going to cover your surface with, this can be:
    • Napkins (Only use the top ply.)
    • Wrapping paper
    • Scrapbooking paper
    • Tissue paper
    • Doilies
    • Extra thin book pages
    • Sewing patterns
    And the list goes on. Thinner materials reduce bubbling and help prevent difficulty with curves.
  6. Water brush pen, scissors, or a blade to tear/cut your material (optional)

  7. Paint brush (Can also be used for tearing)

  8. Container filled with water

  9. Sanding tool
    • Sand paper
    • Sanding sponge, etc.


How to: Reverse Decoupage

  1. Wipe your glass dish with alcohol wipes to get rid of dirt, grime, and oils.

  2. Paint primer on the area behind where you want your decoupage to show through from, including the edges. Do not prime the other side. Let dry overnight.

  3. If you want to apply special effects like paint splatters or glitter, do that now rather than later, otherwise they won’t show through the side with the main design.

  4. Tear or cut your material to the size of your project.
    • Torn edges are easier to blend than cut edges, so if you have a water brush pen or a wet paint brush, you can control the area you want to tear by applying water along the area you intend to tear before tearing.
    • If working on a curved surface, make slits in your material going inwards, towards the raised/lowered area. This helps the material lie flat on the glass dish.
    • If using napkin or a material with multiple plys, only use the ply with the print.

  5. Apply a light coat of your chosen adhesive to both the back of your piece of material and to the area on the dish you’re applying it, then stick on your material, working in sections. Only apply it to the area you’re currently working on.

    • If it’s a large enough section, you can apply the adhesive to a smaller section first and apply that part of the material first to get a better gauge of where to glue.
    • Another reason to apply it in smaller sections is because it dries fast.
    • Applying too much at a time can cause bubbles and wrinkles.
    • If you do get bubbles and wrinkles, you may lightly press on those areas with your brush without adding on any extra adhesive. You may also dab at those areas, smooth, or drag outwards with a squeegee or card, all over saran wrap or a wet sheet of plastic.
    • Be sure to apply the adhesive to the seams and edges of your material to help stabilize it.

  6. Continue until you completely cover your object. Sand off excess material on the edges. Let it dry completely. (Typically at least 15-20 minutes.)


  7. Paint a layer of varnish on the whole decoupaged area. This prevents bleeding should you decide to continue with the next step.

  8. OPTIONAL: Apply a layer or two of white chalk paint to make the colours of your decoupage more vibrant. Apply varnish/adhesive again and let dry.

  9. Put a design of your choice on the back. This step has a lot of room for customization and you can add metal flakes, paint, more paper, glitter, etc.

  10. Apply three more coats of varnish. Wait around an hour in between coats or as long as it takes to dry.

  11. Wait 2-3 days for your piece to cure before use.
